Elon Musk is about to become the world's first trillionaire, and he's going to spend it leaving the planet. The [SpaceX IPO](https://www.irishtimes.com/business/2026/06/03/heavily-in-debt-loss-making-with-eyes-on-sending-people-to-mars-why-would-anyone-invest-in-spacex/) opens to the public this summer — which means, for the price of a share, the rest of us get to help pay for the ticket.

I can't judge him. His father and his classmates [beat him so mercilessly](https://fortune.com/well/2023/09/17/does-elon-musk-have-ptsd-walter-isaacson-biography/) that peace, to him, came to look like an uninhabitable rock 140 million miles away.

So instead I feel sorry for him. Because when he gets to Mars — or the moon, or whatever private island comes next — the air will be different, the rocks will be different, the temperature will be different, and he will be exactly the same. The thing he's running from doesn't need oxygen. It will chase him to his grave, and only there will he see how many people he loved he hurt trying to outrun it.

There's a dark joke in it. Solomon, after a lifetime of building everything a person could build, called it all ["chasing the wind,"](https://www.biblegateway.com/verse/en/Ecclesiastes%201%3A14) and told the rest of us to do simple things instead: plant something, enjoy your family. At least the wind Solomon chased was breathable. Elon's headed somewhere the wind would kill you.

The story isn't new. It's the oldest one we have.

And the resolution isn't "be present" or "count your blessings" — that's the gift-shop version. It's this: there is no Mars. The self is the one address you never move out of, and that isn't a sentence, it's the whole point. It's the only ground solid enough to build a life on.

We all have our version of the rock 140 million miles away — Mars, work, achievement, the next distraction — and we all have our reasons. But the thing you're running toward was never the cure. The life you're running from is the only one there is, and embracing it, difficulties and all, is the only thing that ever made it mean anything.

So when you catch yourself reaching for the exit, don't correct it. Just remember there's no door there. There never was. Only the ground under you, and the people standing on it with you.
